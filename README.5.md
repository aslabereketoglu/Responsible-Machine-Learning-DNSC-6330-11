Part 1 :PGD Evasion Audit: Run the PGD attack across
ε ∈ {0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0} on both the LR and GBT models. For each
model and ε: report FPR by race, AIR, and the ε at which AIR
crosses 0.80. In one paragraph, explain whether the two models are
equally vulnerable and what this implies for model selection in
high-stakes deployments. 

The PGD evasion audit shows that both the Logistic Regression (LR) and Gradient Boosting Tree (GBT) models become increasingly vulnerable as the perturbation budget ε increases. For LR, the false positive rate (FPR) for African-American defendants increases from 0.569 at ε = 0.25 to 1.000 at ε = 2.0, while the Caucasian FPR increases from 0.370 to 1.000. For GBT, the African-American FPR increases from 0.553 to 1.000 and the Caucasian FPR increases from 0.351 to 1.000 over the same ε range. AIR remains above 1.0 throughout the experiment and never crosses below the 0.80 threshold for either model, meaning neither model violates the 4/5ths fairness rule under the tested perturbation budgets. However, this does not imply robustness or fairness preservation. As ε increases, both models converge toward catastrophic behavior where nearly all defendants are classified as high-risk, producing FPR values of 1.000 for both racial groups. The LR and GBT models therefore appear similarly vulnerable overall, although LR degrades slightly faster at lower ε values because its linear structure exposes a more directly exploitable gradient direction. These results imply that model selection in high-stakes deployments should not rely only on clean accuracy or baseline fairness metrics. Even when AIR does not formally cross below 0.80, adversarial perturbations can still dramatically increase false positives and destabilize decision quality across demographic groups. This justifies adversarial robustness testing and continuous fairness monitoring before deployment

Part 2: Poisoning Loop with Fairness Monitoring: Extend the label-flip
poisoning loop to target Caucasian defendants instead of
African-American. (a) Plot AUC and AIR degradation curves for
both target-race variants on the same axes. (b) Identify the “stealth
zone”: the range of poison rates where AUC declines by ≤ 2 pp while
AIR moves outside [0.80, 1.25]. (c) Would a PSI-based drift monitor
(threshold < 0.10 on each feature) detect either attack? Justify with
evidence from your output.

The poisoning experiments show that fairness can degrade substantially even when overall predictive performance remains nearly unchanged. Across both target-race variants, the test AUC stays very stable, fluctuating only between approximately 0.730 and 0.735, meaning the maximum AUC degradation is well below the 2 percentage point threshold. However, AIR moves far outside the acceptable fairness interval of [0.80, 1.25] throughout the poisoning sweep. When African-American defendants are targeted, AIR increases dramatically from about 1.96 to over 3.0 as poison rate increases, indicating severe disparity amplification even though the classifier’s aggregate predictive performance appears stable. When Caucasian defendants are targeted, AIR remains consistently elevated around 1.84–2.04, again indicating substantial fairness distortion with almost no visible degradation in AUC. This creates a large “stealth zone” from 0% to 30% poison rate for both attacks, where standard performance metrics would suggest the model is functioning normally despite major group-level fairness harms. A PSI-based drift monitor with a threshold below 0.10 on each feature would likely fail to detect either attack because the poisoning modifies labels rather than feature distributions. Since the feature values remain essentially unchanged, PSI which measures shifts in input distributions would remain low even while the decision boundary and fairness behavior are being corrupted. These results demonstrate that traditional drift monitoring alone is insufficient for adversarial robustness and must be supplemented with fairness monitoring, label-integrity checks, and security auditing.

Part 3 Membership Inference Depth: Compute the shadow-model MI
AUC for both the LR and GBT models. (a) Plot the confidence-gap
histogram for each model side by side. (b) Test whether the
generalization gap (train AUC − test AUC) predicts the MI AUC
across the two models. (c) Add L2 regularization to the LR model
(vary C ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 1.0, 10.0}), recompute MI AUC, and plot MI
AUC vs. C. What is the practical tradeoff?

The membership inference experiments show that neither the Logistic Regression (LR) nor the Gradient Boosting Tree (GBT) model exhibits substantial privacy leakage under the shadow-model attack. The MI AUC values are approximately 0.497 for LR and 0.501 for GBT, which are essentially equivalent to random guessing (0.50). Although the confidence-gap histograms show that training members sometimes receive slightly higher confidence scores than test non-members, the overlap between the two distributions is very large, preventing the meta-classifier from reliably distinguishing members from non-members. The generalization gap results partially support the hypothesis that larger train–test gaps increase privacy risk: GBT has a larger generalization gap (+0.049) than LR (−0.008) and also has a slightly higher MI AUC. However, the difference is extremely small, suggesting that the observed overfitting is insufficient to produce meaningful membership leakage in this setting. The L2 regularization sweep further shows that changing the LR regularization strength across C ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 1.0, 10.0} has minimal effect on MI AUC, which remains extremely close to 0.50 for all values. This indicates that the LR model is already relatively resistant to membership inference attacks and does not strongly memorize individual training samples. The practical tradeoff is therefore limited in this experiment: stronger regularization slightly reduces model flexibility but does not meaningfully improve privacy because privacy leakage is already near random-guessing levels. Overall, these results suggest that the COMPAS models in this setup do not exhibit substantial memorization-based privacy risk, even though other adversarial vulnerabilities such as poisoning and evasion remain significant concerns.
